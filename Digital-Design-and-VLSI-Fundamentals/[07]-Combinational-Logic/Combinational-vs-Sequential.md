## **1. Combinational Logic Circuits**

Combinational circuits are the "straightforward" thinkers of the digital world. Their output depends only on the current inputs applied at that exact moment.

No Memory: They cannot store data or remember past states. As soon as the inputs change, the output changes (after a tiny propagation delay).

No Clock: They operate independently of a timing signal or clock.

Basic Building Blocks: Made entirely of logic gates (AND, OR, NOT, XOR, etc.).

Common Examples:
Adders & Subtractors: Used to perform arithmetic operations in a calculator or CPU.

Multiplexers (MUX): Acts like a digital switch, selecting one of many inputs to forward to a single output.

Decoders/Encoders: Used to convert data from one format to another (e.g., binary to a 7-segment display).

## **2. Sequential Logic Circuits**

Sequential circuits are the "storytellers." Their output depends not only on the current inputs, but also on the past history of the circuit.

Memory Included: They contain memory elements that store the "state" of the circuit.

Feedback Loop: They feed the output of the circuit back into the input via memory elements.

Clock Driven (Usually): Most sequential circuits are synchronous, meaning they change states only when triggered by a clock pulse.

Basic Building Blocks: Flip-flops and latches.
